The Automatic Stay in New York Bankruptcy

The moment you file bankruptcy, a powerful federal court order called the automatic stay stops nearly all creditor actions against you -- collections, lawsuits, garnishments, foreclosures, and repossessions.

What the Automatic Stay Stops

Under 11 U.S.C. section 362, filing a bankruptcy petition immediately halts:

  • Wage garnishments -- New York allows garnishment of 10% of gross wages or 25% of disposable income (whichever is less). The stay stops this immediately.
  • Foreclosure proceedings -- New York uses judicial foreclosure, which typically takes 12-18 months. The stay halts this process at any stage.
  • Vehicle repossession -- Creditors cannot repossess your car once the stay is in effect.
  • Lawsuits -- All pending civil suits for debt collection are paused.
  • Collection calls and letters -- Creditors must stop all contact.
  • Bank account levies -- Frozen accounts are released.
  • Utility disconnections -- Utilities cannot shut off service for at least 20 days after filing.
  • Eviction proceedings -- May be paused in some circumstances (limited protection).

What the Automatic Stay Does NOT Stop

Certain actions continue despite the automatic stay:

  • Criminal proceedings -- Pending criminal cases continue normally.
  • Child support and alimony collection -- Domestic support obligations are not stayed.
  • Tax audits -- The IRS can continue auditing (but cannot collect).
  • Pension loan repayments -- Withholding from pension/retirement continues.
  • Eviction with judgment -- If a landlord already has a judgment for possession, the stay may not help.

How It Works for New York Filers

Chapter 7

The stay lasts for the duration of your Chapter 7 case -- typically 3-4 months. After discharge, the discharge injunction permanently prevents creditors from collecting discharged debts.

Chapter 13

The stay lasts throughout your entire 3-5 year repayment plan. This gives you time to catch up on mortgage payments through the plan while keeping your home.

Repeat Filers: Limited Stay Rules

If you had a prior bankruptcy case dismissed within the past year, special rules apply under 11 U.S.C. section 362(c):

  • One prior dismissal in the last year: The automatic stay expires after 30 days unless you file a motion to extend it and demonstrate good faith.
  • Two or more prior dismissals in the last year: No automatic stay at all -- you must file a motion and obtain a court order imposing the stay.

Creditor Violations of the Stay

Any creditor who knowingly violates the automatic stay can face serious consequences:

  • Contempt of court -- The bankruptcy court can hold violators in contempt.
  • Actual damages -- You can recover the financial harm caused by the violation.
  • Emotional distress damages -- In cases of willful violation.
  • Attorney fees -- The violating creditor may be required to pay your legal costs.
  • Punitive damages -- In egregious cases, courts may impose additional penalties.

Common violations in New York include continued wage garnishment after filing, repossession attempts, and collection calls. If a creditor violates the stay, notify your attorney (or the court if you are pro se) immediately.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the automatic stay in bankruptcy?

The automatic stay is a federal court order under 11 U.S.C. section 362 that takes effect the instant you file your bankruptcy petition. It stops most creditor actions including lawsuits, wage garnishments, foreclosure sales, repossessions, and collection calls.

Does the automatic stay stop foreclosure in New York?

Yes. The stay immediately halts foreclosure proceedings, including scheduled sales. New York uses judicial foreclosure, which typically takes 12-18 months. Filing bankruptcy stops this process and gives you time to catch up on payments through a Chapter 13 plan.

How long does the automatic stay last?

In a standard first-time filing, the stay lasts throughout your entire bankruptcy case. In Chapter 7, that is typically 3-4 months. In Chapter 13, it lasts for the full 3-5 year plan. If you had a case dismissed within the past year, the stay may be limited to 30 days.

Can creditors violate the automatic stay?

Any creditor who continues collection activity after being notified of the filing violates the stay and may be held in contempt. Courts can award actual damages, attorney fees, and punitive damages for willful violations.
