Foreclosure and Bankruptcy in New York

New York uses judicial foreclosure, which typically takes 12-18 months. Filing bankruptcy stops foreclosure immediately and can give you time to catch up on payments or negotiate alternatives.

New York Foreclosure Process

New York is a judicial foreclosure state, meaning the lender must file a lawsuit in state court. This provides significant protections and time for homeowners:

Day 1-90

Pre-Foreclosure Notice

Lender sends a 90-day pre-foreclosure notice under RPAPL section 1304. This notice must include information about housing counseling services.

Month 3-6

Complaint Filed

Lender files a foreclosure complaint in New York Supreme Court. You have 20-30 days to file an answer.

Month 6-12

Mandatory Settlement Conference

Under CPLR 3408, the court holds mandatory settlement conferences to explore loan modification and other alternatives.

Month 12-18+

Judgment and Sale

If no resolution, the court enters a judgment of foreclosure and sale. A referee conducts the auction. The entire process often takes 18+ months in New York.

How Bankruptcy Stops Foreclosure

Filing bankruptcy triggers the automatic stay under 11 U.S.C. section 362, which immediately halts the foreclosure process at whatever stage it is in:

  • Before the complaint: The lender cannot file the foreclosure lawsuit.
  • During litigation: The lawsuit is paused.
  • After judgment: The scheduled sale is stopped.
  • Day of sale: Even on the day of the auction, filing stops the sale (though timing is critical).

Chapter 13: Save Your Home

Chapter 13 is the most powerful tool for saving a home from foreclosure:

  • Cure arrears over 3-5 years: Missed mortgage payments are spread over the plan period. You pay a portion of the arrears each month in addition to your regular mortgage payment.
  • Regular payments continue: You must stay current on ongoing mortgage payments throughout the plan.
  • Strip second liens: If your home is worth less than your first mortgage, a second mortgage or HELOC can be classified as unsecured and potentially discharged.
  • Modify other debts: Credit cards, medical bills, and other unsecured debts are reduced, freeing income for mortgage payments.

Chapter 7 and Foreclosure

Chapter 7 provides temporary relief but does not permanently solve foreclosure:

  • The automatic stay temporarily stops foreclosure (3-4 months).
  • Personal liability for the mortgage debt can be discharged (no deficiency judgment).
  • But Chapter 7 does not provide a mechanism to catch up on missed payments.
  • After the stay lifts, the lender can resume foreclosure.

Chapter 7 is useful if you plan to surrender the home and want to eliminate the mortgage debt without a deficiency balance, or if you need a few months of breathing room while making other arrangements.

Deficiency Judgments in New York

If a foreclosure sale does not cover the full mortgage balance, the lender can seek a deficiency judgment in New York (RPAPL section 1371). The lender must file within 90 days of the sale. Bankruptcy can eliminate this deficiency:

  • Chapter 7: The deficiency is discharged as unsecured debt.
  • Chapter 13: The deficiency is treated as general unsecured debt in the plan, typically paid pennies on the dollar.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can bankruptcy stop foreclosure in New York?

Yes. Filing bankruptcy immediately stops foreclosure through the automatic stay. Chapter 13 lets you catch up on missed payments over 3-5 years while keeping your home.

How long does foreclosure take in New York?

New York uses judicial foreclosure, typically taking 12-18+ months. Mandatory settlement conferences and court backlogs often extend the timeline further.

Can I save my home with Chapter 13?

Yes. Chapter 13 allows you to cure mortgage arrears over 3-5 years while making regular monthly payments going forward. The automatic stay protects your home during the entire plan. Learn more.

What about the deficiency if my home sells for less than I owe?

New York allows deficiency judgments (lender must file within 90 days of sale). Bankruptcy can eliminate this deficiency in Chapter 7 or address it as unsecured debt in Chapter 13.
